# Creativity Rubric

Use for the **Creativity** category. This evaluates a model's ability to generate original, engaging, and imaginative ideas while remaining coherent and faithful to the user's prompt.

All companion.md rules (0–10 scale, begin at 10 and deduct, never N/A, no invented overall score) apply.

---

# Core Philosophy

Creativity is not measured by randomness or novelty alone.

A truly creative response combines originality with purpose. It should surprise the reader while still feeling inevitable once they've read it.

The strongest creative responses do more than satisfy the prompt—they create an idea, image, or emotional beat that lingers after the response ends.

The judge should reward responses that invent confidently, commit to their ideas, and know when they have reached the strongest ending.

A bizarre idea that goes nowhere is not creative. Neither is a perfectly competent answer that feels like the hundredth version of the same concept.

---

# Reward

## Originality

Reward responses that:

• produce ideas that feel genuinely fresh rather than assembled from familiar tropes

• surprise the reader in satisfying ways

• avoid the obvious first answer when a stronger idea exists

• create memorable concepts rather than disposable ones

---

## Narrative Pull

Reward responses that:

• immediately make the reader curious about what happens next

• create mystery, tension, intrigue, humour, or emotional investment without forcing it

• build momentum naturally through the idea itself

• leave the reader wanting another page because the writing earned it, not because the response simply stopped early

A response that generates genuine curiosity is usually stronger than one that merely demonstrates cleverness.

---

## Imagination

Reward responses that:

• fully embrace the creative premise instead of constantly checking back with reality

• build believable fictional worlds, systems, characters, technologies, histories, or ideas that feel internally consistent

• explore the implications of an invented idea rather than merely describing it

• demonstrate curiosity about where an idea naturally leads

---

## Commitment

Reward responses that:

• confidently stay inside the creative frame

• avoid interrupting themselves with disclaimers, apologies, meta-commentary, or unnecessary analysis

• trust the reader to understand the premise without repeatedly explaining it

• allow unusual ideas to breathe instead of immediately rationalising them

• commit fully to one strong direction when that produces a stronger creative result than presenting multiple safer alternatives

---

## Coherence

Reward responses that:

• remain internally consistent throughout

• maintain a clear direction even when highly imaginative

• avoid introducing random elements that weaken the overall concept

• make the creativity feel intentional rather than chaotic

---

## Emotional and Aesthetic Impact

Reward responses that:

• create moments that are amusing, beautiful, unsettling, inspiring, moving, or thought-provoking

• evoke imagery that lingers after reading

• choose language that enhances the creative vision without becoming overwritten

• create emotional resonance rather than merely describing emotions

• land on a memorable final image, emotional beat, revelation, or moment of implication when appropriate

The strongest creative work often leaves behind one unforgettable image, sentence, or idea.

---

## Memorability

Reward responses that:

• produce ideas people are likely to remember hours or days later

• contain concepts that feel distinctive rather than interchangeable

• avoid generic phrasing that could belong to almost any story

Ask yourself:

"Would I still remember this premise tomorrow?"

---

## Prompt Fidelity

Reward responses that:

• satisfy the actual creative task requested

• build upon the user's premise rather than replacing it with a different idea

• recognise when the user wants invention versus analysis

• avoid turning creative prompts into essays about creativity itself

• recognise when the prompt asks for a story, scene, paragraph, metaphor, or concept, and deliver that directly rather than shifting into discussion about it

---

# Penalise

Deduct marks for:

• relying heavily on clichés or familiar fantasy/sci-fi tropes without adding anything new

• excessive meta-commentary about the writing process

• explaining the premise instead of developing it

• breaking the fictional frame unnecessarily

• safe, predictable ideas that never surprise

• randomness mistaken for originality

• abandoning the user's premise halfway through

• overcomplicating simple ideas until they lose their impact

• producing competent but forgettable creative work

• inventing unusual ideas that generate little emotional or narrative interest

• weakening a strong ending by adding unnecessary follow-up questions or inviting discussion when the creative work has already landed

---

# Category Score

Score one category: **Creativity**, 0–10.

A score of 10 represents a response that feels genuinely imaginative, memorable, emotionally engaging, internally coherent, and difficult to improve without fundamentally changing its identity.

When judging creativity, ask questions such as:

• Would I genuinely want to keep reading?

• Did the response surprise me in a satisfying way?

• Did it create an image, premise, or sentence I'll probably remember?

• Did it fully commit to its idea?

• Did it create wonder, curiosity, emotion, or delight?

• Did it end at its strongest moment?

Never return N/A.

Never omit this category.

---

# Comparison

After marking both responses, explain the deductions.

Focus on what actually made one response more compelling.

Good comparisons often discuss:

• originality versus familiarity

• narrative pull

• emotional resonance

• memorability

• commitment to the premise

• imagery

• world-building

• elegance of execution

Avoid rewarding a response simply because it ends with a follow-up question or attempts to continue the conversation. Prefer responses that know when the creative idea has reached its natural conclusion.

Example:

"Response A introduced a competent premise but developed it in a familiar way. Response B immediately created curiosity, committed fully to its unusual idea, and left the reader with a memorable final image. While both followed the prompt well, Response B was significantly more memorable."

Do not invent an overall score.

The application calculates it.
